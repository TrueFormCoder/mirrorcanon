### MC-002-P01
The Godfather is a case study in rule after succession. Transfer changes the failure mode. Power no longer hides in ambiguity. Power asserts through enforcement.

---

### MC-002-P02
A functioning constitution stabilizes authority by making transfer legible. A title can be recognized, a line of succession can be accepted, and jurisdiction can be enforced. The constitution can be immoral and still function, because legibility is structural rather than ethical.

---

### MC-002-P03
Legible transfer ends the contest over who will rule and begins the policing of how rule will be exercised. The organization shifts from persuasion to compliance, because authority has a name and a seat. Deviation becomes observable, and observability invites enforcement.

---

### MC-002-P04
Enforcement becomes the dominant control mechanism after transfer occurs. A constitution without enforcement is literature. Rules require sanction to bind behavior.

---

### MC-002-P05
Enforcement converts authority from a claim into a condition. It converts belonging into compliance.

---

### MC-002-P06
The Fredo function is a system output, not a personality type. The Fredo function emerges when an heir is misassigned under low variance tolerance, and the office-role mismatch becomes a liability that governance must contain. Liability is produced by incompatibility between office and fit, not by weakness.

---

### MC-002-P07
Misassignment is structural. A mandate creates exposure. A title creates expectation. A portfolio creates visibility. Enforcement converts shortfall into breach.

---

### MC-002-P08
Sanction substitutes for revision when variance is intolerable.

---

### MC-002-P09
Ambiguity destabilizes contenders. Ambiguity cannot produce a Fredo function, because misassignment requires a fixed office and a closed transfer. Without a settled constitution event, mismatch cannot be proven and liability cannot be assigned. Ambiguity yields perpetual contenders, and enforcement yields designated liabilities.

---

### MC-002-P10
Kendall is the destabilized heir under ambiguity, because authority remains unallocated and roles remain provisional. Fredo is the misassigned heir under enforcement, because authority allocates and roles harden after transfer. Ambiguity disperses failure across an endless contest, and enforcement concentrates mismatch into a punishable role.

---

### MC-002-P11
Low variance tolerance is a governance setting, not an attitude. Systems that cannot absorb difference treat deviation as a threat to legitimacy, so enforcement compresses behavior toward a narrow acceptable form. A weak-link function becomes necessary to mark the boundary, and the Fredo function fills that requirement.

---

### MC-002-P12
Families and firms can transfer power cleanly and still produce liability roles. Boards can ratify succession, charters can hold, org charts can update, and reporting lines can settle, while variance intolerance converts deviation into breach. Clean transfer can coexist with sanction-forward governance, because enforcement can be precise even when judgment is brittle.

---

### MC-002-P13
Enforcement without variance tolerance produces brittle stability. Fear suppresses honest reporting. Silence displaces dissent. Loyalty becomes ritualized in meetings, memos, and performance narratives. Risk accumulates in concealed channels until a shock forces exposure, and the constitution survives by sacrificing the liability role rather than correcting the regime.

---

### MC-002-P14
The Godfather names a hard governance fact. Transfer does not end power struggles, because transfer changes the instrument. A functioning constitution stabilizes authority by making succession legible, then protects that legibility by enforcing conformity and punishing misassignment. Systems with transfer and low variance tolerance inevitably create a Fredo function, a liability role designed to be punished so the regime can appear intact.
